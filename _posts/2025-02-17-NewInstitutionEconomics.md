# Course Introduction

<mark>Course Number:09010500 Credits:3</mark>

The teaching content of this course is as follows:   

<table border="1" width="100%">
  <tr>
    <th>Week</th>
    <th>Content</th>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>1-2: Origin of New Institutional Economics</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The emergence and development of New Institutional Economics</li>
        <li>The realistic background of the emergence and development of New Institutional Economics</li>
        <li>The theoretical origin of New Institutional Economics</li>
        <li>The theoretical framework and research methods of New Institutional Economics</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>3-4: Transaction Cost Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The definition of transaction and transaction cost</li>
        <li>The nature and measurement of transaction costs</li>
        <li>The reasons for the existence and changes of transaction costs</li>
        <li>Governance structure and transaction costs</li>
        <li>Vertical integration and transaction costs</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>5-6: Property Rights Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The origin and functions of property rights</li>
        <li>The tragedy of the commons and Coase Theorem</li>
        <li>Property rights theories of Barzel and Alchian</li>
        <li>Property rights theories of Demsetz and Ostrom</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>7-8: State and Constitutional Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The origin and essence of the state</li>
        <li>North's state theory</li>
        <li>State, constitutional government and institutional change</li>
        <li>State, constitutional government and economic growth</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>9: Ideology Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The connotation and characteristics of ideology</li>
        <li>Ideology and social capital</li>
        <li>Ideology and informal institutions</li>
        <li>Ideology and economic growth</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>10-11: Interest Group Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The definition of interest groups</li>
        <li>Interest groups and collective action</li>
        <li>Voting and rent-seeking of interest groups</li>
        <li>Interest groups and institutional change</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>12-13: Path Dependence Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The origin of path dependence theory</li>
        <li>North's path dependence theory</li>
        <li>Greif's revolution of "path dependence"</li>
        <li>The breakthrough of path dependence and path creation</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>14-15: Institutional Change Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>The connotation and motivation of institutional change</li>
        <li>Institutional change from different perspectives</li>
        <li>The process of institutional change</li>
        <li>The modes of institutional change</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <td>16-17: Institutional Performance Theory</td>
    <td>
      <ul>
        <li>Reflection on traditional economic growth theory</li>
        <li>North's institutional performance theory</li>
        <li>Greif's institutional performance theory</li>
        <li>Institutional performance: historical and realistic investigation</li>
      </ul>
    </td>
  </tr>
</table>
